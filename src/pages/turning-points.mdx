# Five Turning Points in GPU History

GPUs didn’t evolve in a single leap — they developed through a series of key turning points that gradually shifted them from fixed-function graphics hardware into highly parallel general-purpose compute systems.

Each milestone introduced a new capability: better rendering efficiency, programmability, or specialized acceleration for modern workloads like AI and real-time graphics.

## Key Milestones (Quick Reference)

These summaries provide a quick overview of each turning point.

- **1996 — 3dfx Voodoo Graphics**
  Early consumer 3D acceleration that reduced CPU load in graphics rendering.

- **1999 — GeForce 256**
  First officially named GPU; introduced hardware transform and lighting (T&L).

- **2007 — CUDA**
  Enabled general-purpose programming on GPUs, marking the start of GPGPU computing.

- **2018 — RTX 20 Series**
  Introduced real-time ray tracing and AI-assisted rendering with dedicated hardware.

- **2022–Present — AI GPU Era**
  GPUs became central to large-scale AI training, inference, and data center computing.

---

## Why These Turning Points Matter

Each milestone reflects a deeper shift in how graphics hardware is designed and used:

- GPUs steadily increased their ability to process many operations in parallel
- Fixed-function graphics pipelines evolved into programmable architectures
- Specialized compute units emerged to handle new workloads (like AI and ray tracing)

Over time, GPUs became not just tools for rendering graphics, but general-purpose parallel processors used across science, engineering, and artificial intelligence.
